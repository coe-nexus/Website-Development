PROLOGUE I OF II

The Pre-Monarchical Origins
of Asymmetrical Control

How Elites Gain and Maintain Power: A Structural Analysis

18 minute read

Abstract

Power structures did not begin with crowns and palaces. Those were merely the branding of dynamics already in motion. This prologue examines the archaeological and anthropological evidence for how asymmetrical control emerged from egalitarian societies over roughly 10,000 years before the first formal kingdoms—through storage, debt, and the control of defensible resources.

1. What Kept Humans Equal for Most of History

For approximately 95% of human existence—roughly 300,000 years—our ancestors lived in what anthropologists call "immediate-return" societies. The key insight from research on groups like the BaYaka, Hadza, and various San peoples is that egalitarianism is not a passive state. It is an assertive, dynamic process that depends on a complex of interdependent practices that constantly resist the emergence of hierarchy, dependency, and inequality.

The mechanisms were active and deliberate:

Demand-Sharing

Anyone could demand food from anyone who had it, making hoarding socially impossible. This wasn't charity—it was an enforceable social norm that prevented accumulation.

Mobility

Rather than confront someone who is trying to oblige you to do something, or seeks to exert authority over you, or with whom you have a dispute, you could simply move away. Because adults did not depend on others for access to vital resources, they could easily relocate. This exit option undermined would-be dominators.

Ridicule and Ostracism

People who bragged or tried to assert their wishes or views on others were mercilessly teased, fought, avoided, and—if they persisted—even exiled. Social sanction was swift and effective.

"Equality had to be enforced against the natural primate tendency toward dominance. When conditions changed, those enforcement mechanisms broke down."

The crucial point: This does not mean humans are by nature egalitarian. Rather, the ecological and demographic conditions common to small-scale societies favored the suppression of steep, dominance-based hierarchy. Humans actively fought hierarchy because their circumstances made it possible—and advantageous—to do so.

2. The Trap: Defensible, Clumpable, Storable Resources

Research on Native American hunter-gatherers identified the key variable: the presence of defensible, clumped resources that can be monopolized as a likely determinant of institutionalized hierarchy. When such resources are present, societies exhibit substantial inequality, including slavery.

This explains why the Pacific Northwest Coast peoples developed hierarchies, slavery, and potlatches without agriculture. Inhabitants of temperate rainforests at the nexus of river and sea, these peoples harvested and stored salmon. They warred and took slaves. They lived in towns, some of which exceeded 1,000 people—all before grain cultivation.

The pattern emerges from three conditions:

  1. A resource that can be stored (grain, dried salmon, etc.)
  2. A resource that's clumped in space (a river, a fertile valley, a grove)
  3. A location that can be defended against others

When all three conditions exist, the person or group controlling the bottleneck can begin controlling other people. The storage facility—the granary, the fish-drying rack, the warehouse—becomes the first technology of power.

The Storage Insight

Power begins with the ability to store what others need and release it slower than they can produce it themselves. The person standing between you and the resource extracts value from that position. This pattern, first visible in prehistoric granaries, persists in every extractive system since.

3. The Transition: From Immediate to Delayed Returns

Anthropologist James Woodburn's distinction between "immediate-return" and "delayed-return" societies captures the mechanism precisely:

"The requirement to manage labor during the period in which the yield is being produced results in relations of dependence and authority developing between people to assure that labor is put in at the right times and that those who contribute are recompensed when the yield is obtained. Control over the distribution of vital resources promotes political inequality and hierarchy through the emergence of elites."

This is the fundamental shift: having something others want and controlling its release slower than production. Agriculture didn't merely provide more food—it created a temporal gap that could be exploited. Someone had to manage the storage, the distribution, the accounting. And those who managed extracted.

4. Big Men: Hoarding Through "Generosity"

The Melanesian "Big Man" system shows how accumulation could emerge even in societies without formal hierarchy. The Big Man accumulates both subsistence and prestige goods—pigs, shell money, yam, taro, and other foodstuffs—in order to give away this wealth. He plans and takes charge of rituals of economic redistribution.

This is the key insight: the accumulation looks like generosity. But in the economies of societies like the Trobriand Islanders, gift-giving is not a selfless act. It can be used to accumulate social power.

By astute economic generosity and management, the Big Man secures influence over his kin and neighbors, who become his debtors. He gives you food now; you owe him labor, loyalty, or support later. This is proto-debt without formal accounting—but it creates the same asymmetry that would later be codified in tablets and ledgers.

5. Debt Before Money: The Mesopotamian Evidence

When we finally get written records (around 3200 BCE), the system is already well-developed. David Graeber's research shows that debt and credit historically appeared before money, which itself appeared before barter. This is the opposite of the narrative given in standard economics texts dating back to Adam Smith.

The earliest records reveal that some of the very first written documents that have come down to us are Mesopotamian tablets recording credits and debits, rations issued by temples, money owed for rent of temple lands.

The Mesopotamian economy was dominated by large public institutions—Temples and Palaces—whose bureaucratic administrators effectively created money of account by establishing a fixed equivalent between silver and the staple crop, barley. Debts were calculated in silver, but silver was rarely used in transactions. The temples controlled grain storage, issued rations, tracked debts, and created the first unit of account.

Early records from Mesopotamia, such as the cuneiform tablets of ancient Sumer, document debts owed to temples or state authorities rather than between individuals—illustrating how debt was closely tied to power structures from its very inception.

6. Was It All Banditry?

Economist Mancur Olson's "stationary bandit" theory offers an analytically rigorous account of early kingship. The answer to whether it was all banditry is: largely yes, but with an important refinement.

The roving bandit takes everything and moves on. The stationary bandit realizes he can extract more over time by settling, claiming a territory, and taking a sustainable percentage. He provides "protection" (initially, from himself), and gradually this protection racket becomes institutionalized as kingship.

But the evidence suggests multiple pathways converged:

  1. The protection racket (Olson's bandit)
  2. The storage controller (temple administrators, Big Men)
  3. The debt manager (those who tracked obligations)
  4. The ritual specialist (those who controlled access to the divine)

Often these were the same people or allied groups. The Mesopotamian temple complex combined all four: it stored grain, tracked debts, managed rituals, and had armed men. The priest-king was simultaneously banker, landlord, warlord, and intermediary with the gods.

"The temple complex represents the first convergence of control mechanisms that would define extractive power for millennia: storage, debt, legitimacy, and force."

7. The Critical Insight: What Changed

The research suggests that substantial increases in wealth inequality occurred beginning in the Bronze Age—prior to the formation of states. And importantly, there is little evidence that early farming actually increased productivity in terms of calories available for consumption per hour of labor.

People didn't adopt agriculture because it was better—they were often coerced into it or trapped by circumstances. As archaeologist Gordon Childe noted, the surplus "was initiated when society persuaded or compelled the farmers to produce a surplus of foodstuffs over and above their domestic requirements."

The "persuasion" included debt traps. In early Mesopotamia, farmers would often become so mired in debt that their children would be forced into debt peonage. This created the first systematic mechanism for converting "I owe you one" into permanent subordination.

Some Neolithic communities were "aggressively egalitarian" and actively fought the emerging pattern. But they ultimately lost to the organizational advantages that hierarchy and surplus extraction provided—particularly in warfare.

The Timeline of Extraction's Emergence

  • Before ~12,000 BCE: Active egalitarianism maintained through leveling mechanisms
  • ~12,000-8,000 BCE: Climate changes enable sedentism in resource-rich areas; storage begins
  • ~8,000-4,000 BCE: Agricultural transition; some populations become trapped in debt/surplus cycles while others resist
  • ~4,000-3,000 BCE: First temple complexes, first bureaucratic debt records, first formal kingship emerges from the fusion of these systems
  • ~3,000 BCE onward: The familiar pattern of divine-right monarchy, formalized extraction, and eventually coinage

8. The Persistent Pattern

The pattern that began with granaries and debt tablets—control through intermediation of something others need—is exactly what persists today. The person standing between you and the resource extracts value from that position.

Traditional finance extends this: banks intermediate between savers and borrowers; central banks intermediate between governments and economies; payment processors intermediate between buyers and sellers. Each intermediary captures rent. The institutional clothing changes; the dynamic remains.

Understanding this origin story is essential for analyzing contemporary power structures. The same three conditions—storable resources, spatial concentration, defensibility—explain why control points emerge in any system: financial infrastructure, data platforms, regulatory frameworks, technical standards.

The question for any system—ancient or modern—is whether it contains leveling mechanisms equivalent to what those immediate-return societies had naturally. Without such mechanisms, the economic gravity that pulls toward concentration will reassert itself.

9. Why This Matters

The technology is necessary but not sufficient. Understanding why hoarding-as-control emerged, and what kept it suppressed for most of human history, gives us the conceptual tools to design systems that might actually work differently.

The egalitarian foragers had active leveling mechanisms. What are the leveling mechanisms for modern economies? For digital platforms? For cryptographic systems? These questions can only be answered by understanding the fundamental dynamics that created extractive hierarchies in the first place.

The main series that follows applies these insights to contemporary contexts: structural positioning, information asymmetry, trust networks, institutions, intergenerational transmission, and technology. But all of it rests on the foundation established here—the recognition that power structures are not natural or inevitable, but emerge from specific conditions that can, in principle, be changed.

Core Insight

Hierarchy is not human nature—it is a consequence of specific conditions. When resources could be stored, concentrated, and defended, extraction became possible. When leveling mechanisms broke down, it became inevitable. Understanding these conditions is the first step toward building systems that resist the gravitational pull toward asymmetrical control.
