PROLOGUE II OF II

From Divine Kings to Decentralized Ledgers:
The Continuity of Monetary Control

How Elites Gain and Maintain Power: A Structural Analysis

22 minute read

Abstract

Centralized monetary authority represents an unbroken line from ancient monarchs who minted coins to central banks that create fiat currency—and blockchain technology offers the first credible technical architecture for breaking this pattern. This prologue traces that continuity across five millennia, examines the philosophical traditions that diagnosed the problem, and evaluates whether cryptographic systems can deliver on their promise of genuine alternatives.

1. The Stakes

The connection between ancient priest-kings and modern central banks is neither metaphorical nor hyperbolic: the same fundamental dynamics that allowed Mesopotamian temple complexes to extract surplus from agricultural populations operate in modified form through contemporary monetary policy. What has changed is not the structure of monetary privilege but its institutional clothing.

If monetary systems inherently concentrate power and constrain individual freedom—as critics from Proudhon to Hayek to the cypherpunks argue—then understanding how these systems emerged and evolved is essential to evaluating alternatives. The evidence suggests that money creation has always benefited those closest to its source, whether the king debasing coins to fund wars or central banks conducting quantitative easing that flows first through financial institutions.

Blockchain's architectural innovation lies in attempting to eliminate this privileged position entirely. Whether it succeeds is an empirical question—one that requires understanding the pattern it seeks to break.

2. How the First Kings Extracted Surplus

The emergence of kingship in the 4th-3rd millennia BCE represents humanity's first institutionalization of extractive hierarchy. Archaeological evidence from Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Indus Valley reveals multiple pathways to royal power, but all converged on a common outcome: the concentration of surplus in the hands of rulers.

James C. Scott's archaeological synthesis in Against the Grain (2017) adds crucial detail. States required specific conditions: cereal grains (wheat, barley, millet) that were "visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, and rationable" for taxation. The 4,000-year gap between grain domestication and state emergence suggests agriculture alone didn't create states—coercion did.

Scott documents that many early populations had to be "forced by the state to do hard labor," that city walls served "restriction as much as for protection," and that perhaps as late as 1600 CE, non-state peoples remained "the vast majority of global human population."

Religious Legitimization

Religious legitimization masked extraction as cosmic order. The Sumerian King List opens "after the kingship descended from heaven," establishing divine mandate. Naram-Sin of Akkad (c. 23rd century BCE) became the first Mesopotamian ruler to declare himself divine—coinciding with the first territorial empire. Egyptian pharaohs were considered living gods guaranteeing Ma'at (cosmic order).

Ernst Kantorowicz's landmark The King's Two Bodies (1957) showed how medieval rulers projected themselves as possessing both physical and symbolic bodies, receiving the Holy Spirit at coronation. The king was simultaneously mortal and divine—a pattern that would later transfer to the "divine right" of monetary sovereignty.

Debt as Moral Obligation

David Graeber's anthropological work identifies how debt became the mechanism converting extraction into moral obligation. In Debt: The First 5,000 Years (2011), Graeber argues that when debt remains unpaid, "the logic of hierarchy prevails"—transforming violence-backed extraction into the victim's apparent moral failure.

The earliest debt systems in Sumer (c. 3500 BCE) predate coinage by over two millennia, suggesting credit preceded coins as a control mechanism. The debtor who cannot pay becomes morally subordinate, not merely economically disadvantaged.

3. Royal Seigniorage and Its Modern Transformation

The king's monopoly on minting coins was "one of the most ancient & which has been one of the most profitable prerogatives annex'd to the Crown," as an 1806 paper to Britain's Privy Council noted. Counterfeiting was treason, punishable by death—not because it harmed commerce but because it challenged sovereign power over money creation.

Seigniorage—the profit from issuing money at face value exceeding production costs—represents the oldest form of monetary privilege. The term itself derives from Old French seignorage, "belonging to the seigneur." When kings needed funds beyond taxation capacity, they debased currency.

The Roman denarius declined from nearly pure silver under Augustus (27 BCE) to 0.5% silver content by 265 CE, producing 1,000% inflation. Emperor Caracalla declared: "Nobody should have any money but I, so that I may bestow it upon the soldiers." Philip IV of France's brutal 1295 debasement ended a century of monetary stability and inaugurated two centuries of guerre monétaire across Western Europe.

The Birth of Central Banking

The transition to central banking didn't eliminate this privilege—it institutionalized it. The Bank of England's founding in 1694 emerged directly from royal finance crisis.

After Charles II's "Great Stop of the Exchequer" (1672)—refusing to repay £1.2 million to goldsmith bankers—private capital markets collapsed. William III needed £1.5 million to rebuild the Royal Navy after France's victory at Beachy Head, but goldsmiths refused to lend.

Scottish merchant William Paterson proposed public subscription: £1.2 million raised in 11 days from 1,268 subscribers, lent to government at 8% interest. The subscribers became "The Governor and Company of the Bank of England."

The revolutionary mechanism: government debt became backing for banknotes. The Bank created money "out of nothing," as its own prospectus stated—lenders "would be allowed to secure payment on the national debt by direct taxation of the people." National debt grew from £1.2 million (1694) to £885 million (1815)—largely from compounded interest.

This wasn't aberration but design: Parliament's control of revenue ensured investors trusted loans would be repaid through taxation.

The Nixon Shock

The Nixon Shock of August 15, 1971 completed the transition from commodity to pure fiat money. At a secret Camp David meeting, Nixon suspended dollar-gold convertibility, claiming "the American dollar was a hostage in the hands of international speculators." By 1976, the Jamaica Accords formally ratified floating exchange rates—money backed only by government authority, not precious metals.

The Cantillon Effect

What persisted across this evolution was the Cantillon Effect, named for French-Irish economist Richard Cantillon (1680s-1734): when new money enters an economy, recipients receive it at pre-inflation prices, while by the time it reaches ordinary citizens, prices have risen. Medieval kings and their retinues benefited first from debased coins; today, financial institutions receiving quantitative easing benefit first from new money. The Federal Reserve's M2 money supply rose over 150% since 2008 while real median wages increased less than 10%.

4. Thinkers Who Diagnosed Monetary Control as Unfreedom

Multiple intellectual traditions—Austrian economics, anarchism, and cypherpunk philosophy—converged on identifying centralized monetary control as a fundamental constraint on human freedom, despite different starting premises and proposed solutions.

The Critics of Monetary Authority

Friedrich Hayek (1899-1992)
In Denationalisation of Money (1976), argued government monopoly on money has "no legitimate moral or legal basis." Governments preserved their monopolies "through coercion and by spreading the fiction that coining and printing money was a central prerogative." His assessment: "The history of government management of money has, except for a few happy periods, been one of incessant fraud and deception." Proposed complete privatization—competing private currencies where "only currencies guaranteeing a stable purchasing power would exist" through market discipline.
Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809-1865)
The first self-described anarchist proposed "People's Banks" issuing mutual credit at minimal interest, arguing interest itself constituted exploitation. His critique targeted not just the state but the very structure of monetary rent extraction.
Benjamin Tucker (1854-1939)
Identified the "money monopoly" as the root from which "both the industrial-capitalist and landlordist monopolies grow." Believed that if "any group of people could legally form a 'mutual bank' and issue credit based on any form of collateral they saw fit to accept, the price of credit would fall to the labor cost of the paperwork involved"—less than one percent.
David Graeber (1961-2020)
Connected debt to broader unfreedom. His Bullshit Jobs (2018) identified how "we have become a civilization based on work—not even 'productive work' but work as an end and meaning in itself." Keynes predicted technology would bring 15-hour workweeks; instead, "our response was to make up a raft of new jobs." Diagnosed modern capitalism as "managerial feudalism"—resembling "classic medieval feudalism, displaying the same tendency to create endless hierarchies of lords, vassals, and retainers."
Nick Szabo
Computer scientist, legal scholar, designer of "smart contracts" (1996) and "Bit Gold" (1998). Articulated the connecting principle: "Trusted third parties are security holes." Every intermediary who can surveil, delay, or block transactions represents both a vulnerability and a control point. Saw "reliance on central banks and third parties as a vulnerability in traditional finance, citing how trust-based monetary systems can lead to inflation and other failures."

The Cypherpunk Synthesis

The cypherpunk movement synthesized these critiques into actionable technical philosophy. Timothy May's "Crypto Anarchist Manifesto" (1988) opened with deliberate echo of Marx: "A specter is haunting the modern world, the specter of crypto anarchy." May predicted cryptography would "alter completely the nature of government regulation, the ability to tax and control economic interactions."

Eric Hughes' "Cypherpunk Manifesto" (1993) declared "privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age" and emphasized direct action: "Cypherpunks write code."

5. Bitcoin and Ethereum as Architectural Alternatives

Bitcoin's whitepaper (October 31, 2008) directly addresses the problem of trusted third parties:

"Commerce on the Internet has come to rely almost exclusively on financial institutions serving as trusted third parties to process electronic payments. While the system works well enough for most transactions, it still suffers from the inherent weaknesses of the trust based model... An electronic payment system based on cryptographic proof instead of trust, allowing any two willing parties to transact directly with each other without the need for a trusted third party."

The Genesis Block (January 3, 2009) embedded a political statement: "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks"—connecting Bitcoin's creation directly to institutional financial failure. This wasn't accidental; Satoshi Nakamoto is explicitly listed among cypherpunks, placing Bitcoin in direct lineage from May and Hughes.

Proof-of-Work and Distributed Consensus

Proof-of-work solves the double-spending problem through distributed consensus rather than institutional validation. The network timestamps transactions by hashing them into a chain of hash-based proof-of-work; "the longest chain not only serves as proof of the sequence of events witnessed, but proof that it came from the largest pool of CPU power." No institution validates transactions—cryptographic verification replaces trust.

Ethereum's Extension

Ethereum's EVM extends this architecture to arbitrary computation. Unlike Bitcoin's distributed ledger, Ethereum operates as a "distributed state machine"—a global decentralized computer executing smart contracts consistently across all nodes. Smart contracts are self-executing programs with terms written directly into code: automated execution when conditions are satisfied, no intermediaries required, transparent logic visible and verifiable on blockchain.

DeFi Protocols

DeFi protocols demonstrate concrete alternatives to institutional finance:

  • MakerDAO (2017): Users lock collateral in smart contracts to mint DAI (decentralized stablecoin), governed by DAO with token holders voting on parameters—decentralized lending without traditional banks
  • Uniswap: Decentralized exchange using automated market makers instead of order books; users swap tokens directly from wallets without depositing to centralized exchanges; anyone can contribute liquidity and earn fees
  • Aave: Non-custodial lending/borrowing protocol; introduced flash loans (uncollateralized loans repaid within single transactions)

These protocols embody permissionless peer-to-peer finance: accessible to anyone with internet and crypto wallet, operational 24/7, no credit checks or bank accounts required. The 1.4+ billion unbanked adults globally represent a concrete use case.

Privacy-Preserving Technologies

Privacy-preserving technologies extend the cypherpunk vision. Zero-knowledge proofs allow proving statements without revealing underlying information. Zcash (2016) implemented zk-SNARKs for shielded transactions—fully encrypted on blockchain yet verifiable under network consensus. Monero uses ring signatures and stealth addresses to automatically obscure all transaction amounts, senders, and receivers.

6. The Honest Case Against: Limitations to Acknowledge

The claim that cryptocurrency achieves "separation of money and state" faces serious empirical challenges. Economic forces recreate centralization despite decentralized architecture.

Mining and Staking Concentration

Mining and staking concentration contradicts decentralization ideals. Bitcoin mining requires "substantial capital investment in specialized hardware," creating "a digital divide" that excludes marginalized communities. AntPool and Foundry USA control disproportionate hashrate.

Research documents: "Mining power and cryptocurrency holdings are increasingly concentrated among a small number of entities." Proof-of-stake creates similar dynamics: Ethereum became more concentrated after transitioning to PoS, and "PoS systems favor wealthier participants, leading to capital oligarchy where larger stakes yield disproportionate rewards and governance control."

Governance Token Concentration

Governance token concentration mirrors traditional wealth inequality. Academic research finds "DeFi's governance is timocratic"—token-holders "accumulate more power over time by purchasing even more tokens." Studies of Compound, Uniswap, and ENS DAOs found "the majority of voting power is concentrated in the hands of a small number of addresses."

Initial token allocations favor founders, investors, and insiders; low voter turnout (often less than 10% of eligible holders) exacerbates power concentration.

"Code Is Law" Limitations

"Code is law" proves insufficient when code contains bugs. The DAO hack (2016) exploited vulnerabilities to drain $50M+ of $168M invested. Ethereum's hard fork to reverse the theft violated the very principle of immutability the ecosystem claimed to uphold.

Recent cases—Indexed Finance (2021), Mango Markets (2022)—demonstrate that "interacting with the smart contract according to its very own publicly available rules" provides no legal defense against fraud charges.

Technical Barriers

Technical barriers exclude those the technology claims to serve. The complexity of navigating blockchain protocols creates "opaque structures of centralized power that may be less accountable than traditional financial institutions." DAOs increasingly adopt "legal wrappers" (LLCs, foundations, trusts)—practical accommodation that some view as betrayal of decentralization ideals.

Critical Assessment

The blockchain ecosystem has created meaningful alternatives for specific use cases—particularly for populations in unstable monetary regimes—but hasn't achieved the wholesale transformation envisioned. The economic dynamics that produce centralization in traditional systems reassert themselves in new technical architectures. The architecture can eliminate institutional intermediaries; it cannot eliminate the human tendency to accumulate advantage.

7. The Enduring Question: Who Creates Money?

The historical through-line from Mesopotamian priest-kings to the Federal Reserve traces a single persistent question: who captures the benefits of money creation?

Olson's stationary bandits became divine-right monarchs who became central bankers—the institutional form evolved but the fundamental dynamic remained. Those closest to money's source benefit first and most; those furthest receive it after prices adjust.

The cypherpunk tradition correctly diagnosed trusted third parties as security holes—not merely in the technical sense but as points where power concentrates and extraction occurs. Szabo's principle connects the Royal Mint debasing coins for medieval warfare to quantitative easing flowing first through Wall Street: both represent institutional proximity to money creation translating into economic advantage.

Blockchain's Genuine Innovation

Blockchain's genuine innovation lies not in eliminating trust but in distributing it across cryptographic verification rather than concentrating it in institutions. Bitcoin's proof-of-work, Ethereum's EVM, and DeFi protocols demonstrate that peer-to-peer financial infrastructure is technically feasible.

Whether this technical possibility can resist the economic gravity that pulls toward concentration—the same gravity that transformed village headmen into divine kings and mutual banks into the Bank of England—remains the open question.

8. Conclusion: Necessary But Not Sufficient

The evidence suggests blockchain offers necessary but not sufficient conditions for monetary freedom. The architecture can eliminate institutional intermediaries; it cannot eliminate the human tendency to accumulate advantage.

Understanding this limitation honestly—while recognizing the genuine alternative that cryptographic systems provide—offers the strongest foundation for building systems that might finally break the pattern that began when the first kings descended from heaven.

What the egalitarian foragers had were active leveling mechanisms—demand-sharing, mobility, social sanction. The question for any new monetary system is: what are its leveling mechanisms? How does it resist the gravitational pull toward concentration that has characterized every monetary system since the first temple granaries?

The technology is necessary but not sufficient. Understanding why monetary control emerged, and what kept it suppressed for most of human history, gives us the conceptual tools to design systems that might actually work differently.
