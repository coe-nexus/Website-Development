Technology and Infrastructure
as Contemporary Power Multipliers

How Elites Gain and Maintain Power: A Structural Analysis

14 minute read

Abstract

Technology creates leverage when it solves coordination problems. This article examines why infrastructure technologies—payments, identity, data systems—create disproportionate influence, distinguishes ethical from extractive technological systems, and explores how to become useful to powerful institutions without becoming subservient to them.

1. Technology as Modern Infrastructure

Throughout this series, we have examined power through the lens of structural positioning—controlling the systems through which others must operate. In contemporary contexts, technology has become the dominant form of such infrastructure.

Just as medieval power often derived from control of physical infrastructure—bridges, ports, trade routes—modern power increasingly derives from control of technological infrastructure: payment systems, communication platforms, data networks, identity verification, and the protocols that enable coordination at scale.

Understanding this technological dimension of power is essential for anyone seeking to navigate or build influence in contemporary systems.

"Software is eating the world."
— Marc Andreessen

Andreessen's observation captures something important: software-based systems increasingly mediate economic and social coordination. Those who build and control these systems occupy structural positions comparable to those who controlled physical infrastructure in earlier eras.

2. Why Infrastructure Tech Creates Influence

Not all technology creates structural power. Consumer applications that compete for user attention operate differently from infrastructure technologies that enable other applications to function. Understanding this distinction is crucial.

Characteristics of Infrastructure Technology

  • Other systems depend on it: Infrastructure becomes a foundation that others build upon, creating switching costs and dependencies
  • Network effects compound: Each additional user makes the system more valuable, creating winner-take-most dynamics
  • Interoperability requirements lock in: Once systems integrate with infrastructure, changing becomes costly and complex
  • Standards become difficult to change: Established protocols persist because coordination to change them is difficult

Examples of Infrastructure Power

Payment systems: Visa and Mastercard process trillions in transactions annually. Every merchant who accepts cards depends on their infrastructure. This dependency creates structural power that transcends any single transaction.

Cloud platforms: AWS, Azure, and GCP host the applications that power modern business. Migration between cloud providers is complex and costly, creating durable dependencies that compound over time.

Identity systems: Control over identity verification—who can prove they are who they claim to be—is increasingly valuable as digital interactions grow. Those who control identity infrastructure control access.

Developer platforms: Operating systems, programming languages, and development frameworks shape what applications are possible and how they're built. Decisions made by platform owners ripple through entire ecosystems.

3. Ethical vs. Extractive Systems

Technology's capacity to create structural power raises important ethical questions. The same infrastructure position can be used to serve users or to exploit them. Understanding this distinction matters both for ethical reasoning and for predicting which systems will endure.

Extractive Systems

Some technological systems maximize value extraction from their structural position. Characteristics include:

  • Charging the maximum the market will bear, regardless of costs
  • Creating artificial dependencies through proprietary lock-in
  • Degrading service quality once dependency is established
  • Prioritizing shareholder returns over user welfare
  • Using data against users' interests

Extractive systems can be highly profitable in the short term but tend to generate resistance, regulation, and eventually competition designed to break their power.

Ethical Systems

Other technological systems use their structural position to create mutual value. Characteristics include:

  • Pricing that reflects costs and reasonable returns rather than maximum extraction
  • Interoperability that allows users to exit if needed
  • Service improvements that compound over time
  • Transparent governance and predictable behavior
  • Data practices that respect user interests

Ethical systems may grow more slowly but often prove more durable. Users and regulators tolerate their structural position because it provides genuine value rather than merely extracting it.

The Ethics-Durability Connection

Ethical behavior is not merely morally preferable but often strategically superior. Systems that create genuine value accumulate trust and tolerance for their structural position. Systems that extract value generate resistance that eventually limits their power.

4. Why AI Rewards Builders, Not Hype Merchants

The current wave of artificial intelligence development illustrates broader principles about technology and power. Understanding who will gain lasting influence from AI requires distinguishing different forms of participation.

Hype vs. Infrastructure

Media attention focuses on dramatic predictions and consumer-facing applications. But durable power in AI is likely to accrue to those building infrastructure:

  • Compute infrastructure: Those who control AI training and inference capacity
  • Data pipelines: Those who aggregate and structure training data
  • Model architecture: Those who design foundational models others build upon
  • Development tools: Those who create the frameworks and libraries developers use
  • Deployment platforms: Those who provide the infrastructure for running AI systems at scale

Tool-Builders vs. Tool-Users

Those who build AI tools gain different advantages than those who use them. Users may gain temporary competitive advantages from AI capabilities, but these advantages tend to erode as AI tools become widely available. Tool-builders accumulate structural power as others depend on their systems.

This suggests prioritizing AI-related investments in building rather than merely using—developing capabilities that others will need rather than simply deploying capabilities others have built.

5. Becoming Useful Without Serving Blindly

A practical challenge for individuals: how to become useful to powerful institutions—gaining access, resources, and influence—without becoming subservient to their agendas.

The Usefulness Trap

Institutions value people who solve their problems. This creates opportunities for those who can provide such solutions. But simple usefulness can become dependency. The person who is merely useful to an institution has no leverage—they can be replaced by anyone else equally useful.

Strategic Usefulness

More sustainable positions combine usefulness with distinctiveness:

  • Develop rare expertise: Be useful in ways few others can be. Scarcity creates leverage.
  • Maintain multiple relationships: Usefulness to multiple institutions prevents dependency on any single one.
  • Build independent reputation: Recognition that exists independent of any institution creates options.
  • Create rather than just serve: Those who build things have assets; those who only serve have relationships.
  • Preserve optionality: Avoid arrangements that foreclose future choices even when they offer short-term benefits.

The Independence Principle

The goal is to be useful enough to gain access and influence while remaining independent enough to maintain integrity and options. This balance requires conscious attention—the pull toward full absorption into institutional agendas is strong for those who are genuinely useful.

"The measure of a man is what he does with power."
— Plato

6. Building Technical Leverage: Practical Principles

Principle 1: Build Infrastructure, Not Just Applications

When possible, build systems that others depend on rather than applications that compete within existing ecosystems. The infrastructure builder accumulates structural power; the application builder faces continuous competition.

Principle 2: Solve Coordination Problems

The most valuable technologies solve coordination problems—enabling cooperation that was previously impossible or prohibitively expensive. Look for friction in coordination and build tools to reduce it.

Principle 3: Create Network Effects

Design systems that become more valuable as more people use them. Network effects create sustainable advantages that are difficult to displace. Single-user utility is less defensible than multi-user utility.

Principle 4: Invest in Standards

Participation in standards development—whether formal standards bodies or de facto standards through open-source projects—creates influence over the infrastructure that shapes entire industries.

Principle 5: Understand the Full Stack

Power in technology often flows to those who understand how pieces fit together. Deep expertise in one layer combined with broad understanding of adjacent layers creates disproportionate leverage.

7. The Historical Continuity

Contemporary technological power represents continuity, not discontinuity, with historical patterns. The mechanisms described in Part I—controlling the systems through which others must operate—apply with equal force to digital infrastructure.

What has changed is the medium, not the dynamic. Medieval merchant families controlled trade routes; modern technology companies control data routes. Renaissance bankers controlled credit flows; modern payment networks control transaction flows. Industrial-era industrialists controlled supply chains; modern platform companies control digital marketplaces.

Understanding this continuity prevents both technophobic rejection and technoutopian naivety. Technology creates new forms of the same structural power dynamics that have always shaped human organization.

8. Conclusion

Technology creates leverage when it solves coordination problems at scale. Infrastructure technologies—payments, identity, data, platforms—create structural power comparable to historical control of physical infrastructure. Those who build and control these systems occupy positions that shape entire ecosystems of activity.

For individuals, this analysis suggests prioritizing technical capabilities that create infrastructure rather than merely using infrastructure others control. It also suggests navigating relationships with powerful institutions carefully—being useful enough to gain access while maintaining enough independence to preserve integrity and options.

The ethical dimension matters strategically as well as morally. Systems that create genuine value tend to accumulate tolerance for their structural position; systems that merely extract value generate resistance that eventually limits their power.

Core Takeaway

Power accrues to those who reduce friction for others at scale. Technology creates leverage not through complexity or cleverness but through solving coordination problems that were previously unsolvable. Build systems that enable others to coordinate, and influence follows.
